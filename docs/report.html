Overview

A benchmark for evaluating AI biomedical peer review tools

bioreview-bench pairs open-access biomedical articles with the substantive concerns raised during professional peer review. Each concern is annotated with category, severity, and author stance. The benchmark measures how well AI tools identify concerns that human reviewers would raise.

Ranking Under Each GT

F1 scores for 6 baseline models under Haiku GT vs GPT-4o-mini GT. Rankings invert because models generating more concerns are penalized by the smaller GPT GT.

Asymmetric overlap reveals a granularity difference, not a substance disagreement. 97.6% of GPT-4o-mini’s concerns match a Haiku concern (reverse direction), while only 63.9% of Haiku concerns match a GPT concern (forward direction). This means GPT-4o-mini identifies a strict subset of what Haiku finds — the two extractors agree on which concerns exist but disagree on how many to extract.

The leaderboard inversion is a count-calibration artifact. Under GPT GT (6.7 concerns/article), models generating ~15 concerns (Haiku, GPT-4o-mini) incur massive precision penalties from unmatched predictions, while models generating ~10 concerns (Llama, BM25) happen to be better count-calibrated. Kendall’s τ = −0.600 (12/15 pairwise swaps) does not mean the models perform differently — it means F1 rankings depend on the ratio of model output count to GT count.

Discussion

Key findings & limitations

Finding 1: Models cluster into three statistical tiers. Paired bootstrap testing (10,000 resamples, Bonferroni-corrected α ≈ 0.003) reveals three groups: Haiku-4.5 and GPT-4o-mini form the top tier (F1 0.694–0.699, ΔF1 = 0.005, p = 0.003), Gemini-2.5-Flash and BM25 form a middle tier (F1 0.685–0.686, p = 0.56), and Gemini-Flash-Lite and Llama-3.3-70B form the bottom tier (F1 0.653–0.658, p = 0.33).

Finding 2: Performance varies dramatically by source. All models achieve near-perfect recall on eLife (>0.91) but low precision (~0.38–0.55), while PLOS shows the highest F1 (0.67–0.78). This pattern reflects source-specific differences: eLife articles have fewer ground-truth concerns per article, causing models' fixed-count outputs to produce many unmatched predictions.

Finding 3: Statistical methodology concerns are most reliably detected. These concrete, well-defined concerns achieve the highest per-category F1 (0.67–0.78). Writing clarity (F1 0.59–0.70) and reagent/method specificity (F1 0.63–0.71) are harder, likely because they require domain-specific expertise or subjective judgment.

Finding 4: The BM25 retrieval baseline is surprisingly competitive. A simple lexical similarity baseline achieves F1 = 0.685, statistically indistinguishable from Gemini 2.5 Flash (p = 0.56). This suggests many peer review concerns are somewhat predictable from the manuscript text alone.

Finding 5: Threshold choice is not fragile. F1 remains stable from t=0.50 to t=0.80 for all models, with a sharp drop only at t=0.90. The similarity distribution confirms this: 98.5% of matched tool concerns have cosine similarity > 0.85, creating a clear separation from non-matches.

Finding 6: Rankings depend on extractor granularity, not concern substance. Re-extracting all 981 test articles with GPT-4o-mini yields 6.7 concerns/article vs Haiku’s 14.2. Despite this 2.1× volume gap, 97.6% of GPT concerns match a Haiku concern, confirming both extractors identify the same underlying issues. However, model rankings invert under the coarser GT (Kendall’s τ = −0.600): models producing more concerns are penalized by the smaller reference set. Category agreement is moderate (κ = 0.528), with writing_clarity and prior_art_novelty most consistent across extractors. This finding implies that F1 rankings should be interpreted relative to the GT extractor, and that recall (less sensitive to count mismatch) may be a more robust metric for cross-extractor comparisons.

Limitations

  • Silver labels sensitive to extractor granularity. Ground-truth concerns are extracted from peer reviews by Claude Haiku 4.5, not manually annotated. Cross-model validation with GPT-4o-mini shows 97.6% of its concerns match Haiku’s (substance agreement), but Haiku extracts 2.1× more concerns (14.2 vs 6.7 per article). This granularity difference inverts leaderboard rankings under the alternative GT (τ = −0.600). Rankings should be interpreted as relative to the specific GT extractor. BM25’s competitive F1 (0.685) suggests the labels are not pathologically model-specific in substance, but count-dependent metrics like F1 are sensitive to extractor granularity.
  • 944 vs 981 test articles. Tool outputs were generated for 944 test articles from 4 sources (eLife, PLOS, F1000Research, Nature). 37 PeerJ articles in the test split were not evaluated by all models. Per-source PeerJ metrics are available for 5 of 6 models as supplementary data.
  • Nature small sample (n=8). Only 8 Nature articles are in the test split. Per-source results for Nature should be interpreted as preliminary; bootstrap confidence intervals are uninformatively wide.
  • Figure concerns excluded. AI models cannot inspect manuscript figures. The ~5% of concerns requiring figure analysis are filtered from evaluation.
  • Source imbalance. F1000Research dominates the test set (403/981, 41%), while Nature (8) and PeerJ (37) are underrepresented. Per-source breakdowns help contextualize this imbalance.
